Introduction

What this report is about

The classical three cycles, the extended Spiritual cycle, and the model's history

Biorhythm theory proposes that life moves in three independent sine-wave cycles that begin at birth and continue uninterrupted through life — a Physical cycle of 23 days, an Emotional cycle of 28 days, and an Intellectual cycle of 33 days. The model was first articulated by Wilhelm Fliess in the late 1800s, refined by Hermann Swoboda, and given its third (Intellectual) cycle by Alfred Teltscher in the 1920s. Later authors added an extended set — Intuitive (38 days), Aesthetic (43 days), Self-Awareness (48 days), and Spiritual (53 days) — of which the Spiritual cycle has the broadest agreement.

This report uses four cycles: the classical three plus the Spiritual. Each cycle's value at any moment is a number between minus one and plus one — the sine of the elapsed fraction of the cycle. Days near plus one are read as the high phase for that domain; days near minus one as the low; days near zero as the critical days, when the cycle crosses the midline and a brief instability may be felt before the next phase begins.

An honest framing applies to the whole. Biorhythm is an interpretive model and has not been validated in controlled studies. It is offered alongside numerology and palmistry as a traditional framework for self-reflection, not as a clinical instrument. Most readers find its actual value in the structured invitation to notice their own rhythms — to map their lived experience against a simple, regular pattern, and to discover where their own life does and does not respect the model.

Origin
Fliess and Swoboda (late 1800s); Teltscher added the Intellectual cycle in the 1920s; extended cycles followed in later decades.

Structure & method

The chart, the four cycles in turn, the critical days, and the synthesis

This report is structured so that each focus aspect lives on its own page. The first focus presents the biorhythm chart — all four cycles projected forward over ninety days from today. The next four focuses describe each cycle in turn — Physical, Emotional, Intellectual, Spiritual — with its current phase and what to make of it. The sixth focus describes the critical days and how to read them. The seventh is a synthesis with a brief note on the further extended cycles.

Calculations are simple: from your date of birth, 16 May 1953, the elapsed days as of the reading date are approximately 26 658. Each cycle's current phase is the remainder when that day-count is divided by the cycle length. From these phases the curves are extended forward into the year ahead. The reading is therefore profile-agnostic and continues to be valid however one tests it against lived experience.

A standard note applies. Biorhythm describes a pattern; it does not prescribe behaviour. Use the chart as a quiet reference for noticing what is happening rather than as an authority dictating what should happen. The reader is the final authority on the reading.

II · Physical CycleFocus 2 of 7
Focus II · the body's rhythm

The Physical Cycle · 23 days

Stamina, endurance, immune response, and physical confidence

The Physical cycle is the shortest of the four and the most concrete. It governs stamina, endurance, physical strength, and the body's resilience to stress.

On the high half of the cycle, people typically report easier sleep, faster recovery from exertion, stronger appetite, and a steadier baseline of physical energy. This is the half traditionally favoured for athletic events, manual work, surgical procedures elected at the patient's discretion, and any commitment that taxes the body. Travel feels less depleting; physical projects that have stalled often find a new push.

On the low half, the body asks for less. Recovery slows, energy is more easily spent than refilled, and the immune system runs at lower margin. The low half is not a problem so much as a different mode: it is a good time to rest, to consolidate, to delegate physical effort, and to accept that the same hour of exercise costs more than it did a fortnight ago. People who fight their physical low half often find themselves unwell at the end of it.

Your Physical cycle today reads at about plus zero point three of one — recently across the midline, in the first quarter of the high half. The next ten days are favourable for physical commitments. The cycle peaks around five days from now, descends to the midline at around day twelve, and reaches its trough at around day seventeen, at which point recovery and rest move to the top of the agenda.

Focus III · the heart's rhythm

The Emotional Cycle · 28 days

Mood, sensitivity, relational ease, and emotional resilience

The Emotional cycle, at 28 days, is the longest of the classical three. It governs mood, sensitivity, the ease of relating to others, and the resilience of one's emotional baseline.

On the high half, people typically experience a warmer baseline of mood, more easy contact with others, a livelier sense of humour, and a generally more generous reading of difficult situations. The world feels more workable. Difficult conversations land more softly; ambiguous comments are absorbed without strain. This is the half traditionally favoured for important conversations, family events, public-facing engagements, and the patient work of repair in close relationships.

On the low half, the emotional skin is thinner. Things land harder; minor remarks may stay with one longer than they merit; one's own moods may surprise one with their texture. The low half is not a depression in any clinical sense; it is the natural withdrawal of the emotional surplus that buoys the high half. Wisdom in the low is to expect the harder reading, take fewer commitments, and not to take one's own state as the definitive truth about a situation.

Your Emotional cycle today reads at about plus zero point four — climbing solidly toward the high. The next ten to twelve days are favourable for relational and family engagements. The cycle peaks around day six from now, returns to the midline around day fourteen, and reaches its low around day twenty-one.

Focus IV · the mind's rhythm

The Intellectual Cycle · 33 days

Concentration, analysis, memory, and the appetite for new learning

The Intellectual cycle, at 33 days, governs the sharpness of attention, analytical work, memory, and the appetite for taking in new ideas.

On the high half, mental work feels easier and faster. Concentration sustains for longer stretches, memory is more available, and one is more willing to take on intellectually demanding tasks — writing, complex reading, learning a new domain, designing something from scratch. The high intellectual half is the time to do the work that has been put off because it required more focus than one had been ready to give.

On the low half, the mind moves more slowly. Concentration tires sooner; memory is less reliable; familiar tasks may go fine but anything that requires fresh thinking costs more. The low half rewards rest from heavy intellectual demand and is well-suited to administrative work, routine review, and the kind of mental task that runs on previously-learned patterns rather than on freshly-built ones.

Your Intellectual cycle today reads at about minus zero point nine — near the deepest trough of the cycle. The next week is the low point intellectually; recovery toward the midline takes about seven days, and the next peak is reached around day fifteen from today. The chart suggests being kind to the mind for the immediate week ahead — keep the schedule lighter on demanding new thinking, and reserve major intellectual commitments for the second half of the month.

V · Spiritual CycleFocus 5 of 7
Focus V · the inner rhythm

The Spiritual Cycle · 53 days

Inner attunement, contemplative depth, the sense of meaning beyond the day

The Spiritual cycle, at 53 days, is the slowest of the four and the most subtle. It governs the depth of inner attunement, the sense of meaning beyond the day's particulars, and the receptivity to what is quiet.

On the high half, contemplative practices come more easily — meditation, reflective writing, prayer, the silent walk. The sense of being inwardly settled is stronger. One reads life as having a shape rather than only events; one is more available to silence and to the long perspective. This is the half traditionally favoured for retreats, study of contemplative texts, and the kind of inner work that requires a steady ground beneath it.

On the low half, the inner ground feels thinner. The day's events have more grip on attention; contemplative practices may feel mechanical or absent of their usual settling effect; one is more easily caught in surface noise. The low half is not a spiritual failure — it is the rest phase of a deep rhythm, and the wise response is to keep the form of practice without expecting the usual fullness from it. The inner ground returns in its own time.

Your Spiritual cycle today reads at about minus zero point one — just below the midline, transitioning upward. You are emerging from a long low phase and entering the rising half of the cycle. The cycle peaks around day fifteen from today and remains above the midline until about day twenty-six. The window now opening is one of the best in the year ahead for retreat, contemplative writing, or the deepening of any practice.

VI · Critical DaysFocus 6 of 7
Focus VI · the crossings

Critical Days

A moment of passage — brief instability as a cycle moves between its high and low halves

A critical day in biorhythm theory is the day on which a cycle crosses the midline — rising from low to high, or descending from high to low.

On a critical day, the cycle's value is near zero, and the body, heart, or mind associated with it is briefly between phases. The tradition reads this as a moment of slight instability — not bad luck, just absence of usual support. A physical critical day is associated with mild susceptibility to small accidents or fatigue; an emotional critical day with mood volatility; an intellectual critical day with a brief drop in concentration; a spiritual critical day with a passing flat patch in inner life.

For the year ahead, your critical days will fall approximately every twelve days for Physical, every fourteen for Emotional, every seventeen for Intellectual, and every twenty-six for Spiritual. Double critical days — when two cycles cross the midline together — happen occasionally and are read as more noticeable; triple critical days, rare and remembered.

The correct posture toward a critical day is gentle care, not avoidance. Do less on those days, keep the schedule flexible, expect to be slightly less than oneself for a short window. The next physical critical day for you falls around day twelve from now; the next emotional, around day fourteen; the next intellectual, around day seven (rising from the current low); the next spiritual, today or tomorrow as the long low ends and the high half opens.
