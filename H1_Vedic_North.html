Introduction

What this report is about

Jyotish — the sidereal zodiac, the nakshatras, and the dasha cycles of life

Vedic astrology — jyotish, "the science of light" — is one of the six vedangas of classical Indian learning, continuously practised for more than two thousand years.

Its earliest texts are the Vedanga Jyotisha attributed to Lagadha; its later canonical works are the Brihat Parashara Hora Shastra and Varahamihira's Brihat Jataka. Jyotish differs from Western astrology in three essential ways. It uses the sidereal zodiac, fixed against the actual stars rather than the seasons, separated from the tropical zodiac by the ayanamsha — about 23°13' for 1953 by the Lahiri reckoning. It divides the ecliptic into twenty-seven nakshatras, lunar mansions of 13°20' each. And it uses dasha cycles, planetary periods drawn from the Moon's nakshatra, to time the unfolding of the chart over the lifetime.

This report draws your sidereal natal chart for 16 May 1953 at 20:15 Indian Standard Time. It reads the lagna (ascendant) and its nakshatra, the Moon and its nakshatra, the Sun and its nakshatra, the placement of the major grahas in the twelve bhavas, and the current Vimshottari dasha period. The kundali is presented in the North Indian style — the format traditional to North India, in which the twelve houses are fixed in their grid positions and the rasis (signs) rotate according to the lagna.

How to read this report

Structure & method

The kundali first; then lagna, Sun and Moon; then planets and dasha

This report is structured so that each focus aspect lives on its own page. The first focus presents the North Indian kundali; the next three describe the lagna, Sun, and Moon in their nakshatras.

Pages after the angles describe the placement of the major grahas in the bhavas, the Vimshottari dasha system as a whole, and the current dasha period with a synthesis. Positions are calculated for 16 May 1953 at 20:15 Indian Standard Time, which is 14:45 Universal Time, with the Lahiri ayanamsha. The lagna is sensitive to the exact minute of birth.

In the North Indian format, the grid is house-centred: house 1 (the lagna) always occupies the top-second-from-left cell, and the houses proceed clockwise — 2, 3, 4 across the top and down the right, 5, 6, 7, 8 across the bottom (right to left), and 9, 10, 11, 12 up the left side. The rasi (sign) in each house depends on the lagna; for this chart, Tula rises. The traditional planet abbreviations — Su (Surya), Mo (Chandra), Ma (Mangal), Bu (Budha), Gu (Guru), Ve (Shukra), Sa (Shani), Ra (Rahu), Ke (Ketu) — follow North Indian almanac convention.

A standard note applies throughout. Jyotish is interpretive and offered alongside one's own discernment. Remedial measures are best undertaken with qualified consultation rather than from a written report alone. The chart is a frame for reflection and timing; the life is lived by the one whose chart it is.

Focus II · the rising sign

Lagna · Rising Sign in Tula (Libra) · 16°47'

Swati nakshatra · ruled by Rahu, the wind that bends without breaking

The lagna is the rasi rising on the eastern horizon at the moment of birth — the body of the chart, the form within which all other placements are held.

Tula (Libra) is a movable air sign ruled by Shukra (Venus). A Tula lagna gives a refined, balanced, and aesthetically attentive form to the chart. Such natives are read as natural mediators — sensitive to fairness, drawn to art and to right relationship, with a quiet diplomatic instinct that finds the workable middle in most situations.

Your lagna sits in Swati, the fifteenth nakshatra, ruled by Rahu. The symbol of Swati is a young blade of grass moved by wind — the image is independence, flexibility, and the capacity to bend without breaking. Swati natives are read as self-made, intelligent, and unusually skilled at navigating circumstance. They tend to find their own path rather than inherit one, and their authority arrives quietly across time rather than suddenly through position.

The combination of Tula rasi and Swati nakshatra reinforces a single quality — balance found through movement. The chart's body is settled, but settled by responsiveness rather than by stubbornness. It listens, adjusts, and finds the right form for each situation as it arises.

Focus III · the king of the chart

Surya · Sun in Vrishabha (Taurus) · 1°47'

Krittika nakshatra · the cutting flame, the sharp purifier

The Sun in jyotish represents the core self, the soul, and the relationship to authority and to the father.

Vrishabha (Taurus) is a fixed earth sign ruled by Shukra. The Sun here is grounded, patient, and unusually steady — it builds the kind of authority that works through endurance rather than through declaration. Vrishabha Sun natives are read as reliable, sensual, generous to those they have decided to support, and slow to abandon either commitments or grudges.

Your Sun sits in Krittika, the third nakshatra, ruled by Surya itself. Krittika's symbol is a flame, and its presiding deity is Agni — the fire that purifies and clarifies. The Sun in its own nakshatra is unusually empowered. Krittika natives are read as having a discerning, sometimes cutting clarity — the ability to see and to name what is real beneath what is presented. The pada (2) places the Sun in Vrishabha bhaga of Krittika, softening the edge with Taurean steadiness.

The Sun is conjunct Jupiter and Mercury retrograde (also in Vrishabha), forming the classical Surya-Guru conjunction with a reflective Mercury in the same degree cluster — read as the chart of the natural advisor, wisdom and authority working as one. From the Tula lagna, all three planets fall in the eighth house, the bhava of depth, transformation, hidden things, and inheritance. The chart neither fears nor sensationalises what lies beneath the surface.

Focus IV · the inner self

Chandra · Moon in Mithuna (Gemini) · 5°47'

Mrigashira nakshatra pada 4 · the seeker, the gentle questioning mind

The Moon in jyotish is the deepest reading-point of the chart. It represents the mind (manas), the emotional life, and the running tone of consciousness from day to day.

Mithuna (Gemini) is a mutable air sign ruled by Budha (Mercury). The Moon here is curious, communicative, and intellectually awake. Mithuna Chandra natives think their feelings — they process the inner life through language, story, and ideas, and they are most themselves in conversation with people who can keep pace with a moving mind.

Your Moon sits in Mrigashira, the fifth nakshatra, ruled by Mangal (Mars). Mrigashira's symbol is the head of a deer, and its presiding deity is Soma. The image is searching — Mrigashira natives are read as perpetual seekers, gentle and curious by temperament, drawn to investigation, study, and the next horizon. The pada (4), in the Pisces bhaga, deepens this with a contemplative and slightly mystical quality.

The Moon falls in the ninth house from your lagna — the house of dharma, higher learning, the guru, and long journeys both literal and figurative. A Mithuna Moon in the ninth is one of the most fortunate placements in jyotish for a chart oriented toward learning, teaching, and the sustained pursuit of meaning.

Where each planet sits in your twelve-house map of life

Each graha in your chart falls in a particular bhava, describing the area of life through which that planet's energy is most active.

Focus VI · the timing wheel

The Vimshottari Dasha

A 120-year cycle of planetary periods, drawn from the Moon's nakshatra

The Vimshottari dasha system divides life into nine planetary periods totalling 120 years. Each period — a mahadasha — colours the chart with the energy of one graha for many years at a time.

Because your Moon falls in Mrigashira, the first mahadasha at birth was that of Mangal (Mars), the lord of Mrigashira. Mrigashira spans 23°20' Vrishabha to 6°40' Mithuna; your Moon at 5°47' Mithuna is roughly 93% of the way through it. Of the seven-year Mars dasha, therefore, only about five and a half months ran from birth — to roughly November 1953.

The full sequence of mahadashas from birth follows the Vimshottari order: Mars (5.6 months remaining at birth) → Rahu (18 years, to ~November 1971) → Jupiter (16 years, to ~November 1987) → Saturn (19 years, to ~November 2006) → Mercury (17 years, to ~November 2023) → Ketu (7 years, to ~November 2030) → Venus (20 years, to ~November 2050) → Sun (6 years) → Moon (10 years).

Each mahadasha contains internal subperiods (antardashas) of the nine planets, and each antardasha contains further internal periods (pratyantar dashas). The mahadasha sets the long colour; the antardasha sets the colour of a particular two-to-three-year theme within it. Precise sub-period calculations are verified by software using your specific Mars-balance at birth.

Focus VII · the current period & portrait

Ketu Mahadasha · 2023–2030

The seven years of the inward turn — and the unified portrait

You are presently in the Ketu Mahadasha, which began around November 2023 and runs through November 2030.

Ketu, the south node of the Moon, is the planet of moksha — release, detachment, and the inward turn. Ketu mahadashas are read as periods of refinement and renunciation: not necessarily withdrawal from the world, but deciding what is essential and quietly setting aside what is not. The seven Ketu years tend to favour writing, study, contemplative practice, and the consolidation of inheritance. They reward patience and precision; they are unkind to scatter and to reaching outward for new things the chart no longer needs.

The unified Vedic portrait reads as follows. A Tula lagna in Swati nakshatra gives a refined, balanced, self-made form to the chart — one that bends without breaking. The Sun in Krittika (Vrishabha) gives a discerning Taurean authority that builds patiently and sees clearly. The Moon in Mrigashira (Mithuna) in the ninth dharma bhava is the chart's life-long signature — the seeker's mind, oriented to learning, teaching, and meaning. Saturn alone in the twelfth (Kanya) provides the long-arc inner discipline; Ketu in the fourth (Makara) marks a lifetime's detachment from domestic comfort in favour of the wider field. The Surya-Guru-Budha conjunction in the eighth marks the natural advisor and counsellor.

Read together, this is the chart of an elder-counsellor in the dharma line — authority earned by long inward labour, exercised through teaching and counsel, rooted in family and tradition, and presently entering a seven-year period particularly suited to the writing-down and refinement of what has already been learned.
