Introduction

What this report is about

The magic square brought from the river by the great tortoise

Chinese numerology rests on a single elegant figure — the Lo Shu, a three-by-three magic square in which every row, every column, and both diagonals sum to fifteen. The legend has it that the diagram first appeared on the shell of a giant tortoise emerging from the River Lo, and that the sage-emperor Yu the Great read it there and gave it to his people.

The Lo Shu is the foundational diagram of Chinese cosmology. It underlies Feng Shui, the I Ching's eight trigrams, and the entire system of nine palaces through which Chinese tradition reads space, time, and the human person. Each cell in the grid is a palace, each palace is a direction, an element, and a domain of life.

This report places the digits of your date of birth into the nine palaces of the Lo Shu. Numbers that appear, repeat, or are absent describe your temperament; rows, columns, and diagonals fully present form arrows of strength, while those entirely empty form arrows of weakness. The reading is supplemented by your Driver and Conductor numbers, your Feng Shui Kua direction, and the Year Pillar of your birth.

How to read this report

Structure & method

The grid first; then the personal numbers; then the year and the elements

This report is structured so that each focus aspect lives on its own page. That arrangement gives every theme room to breathe, and lets you read in any order, returning to whichever page you wish.

The first focus page presents the Lo Shu grid filled with the digits of your date of birth. The pages that follow read the personal numbers — Driver, Conductor, Kua — that the tradition derives from the same date, then describe your Year Pillar, the arrows of the grid, and the balance of the five elements.

Each focus carries a small icon at the top, and the calculation is shown beneath, set apart by hairlines. A standard note applies to the whole. Lo Shu numerology and Feng Shui Kua are interpretive practices from Chinese tradition. They describe character and tendencies; they do not predict events. The reader is the final authority on the reading.

Reading time
Approximately twenty-five minutes at an unhurried pace.
Authority
Indications, never prescriptions.

II · Driver NumberFocus 2 of 7
Focus II · the engine of the personality

Driver Number 7

命主數 — the Day Number, the inward seeker

The day of birth, reduced, is the Driver Number — the engine of the personality, the part that pulls the chart forward.

For the sixteenth, one plus six gives seven. Seven in the Chinese reading is the number of contemplation, refinement, the inward seeker, and the connoisseur of quality. Sevens prefer depth to breadth and tend to keep counsel until they have something measured to say.

A Driver seven is the engine of someone who studies before speaking and observes before deciding. Such people are the family's quiet expert, the firm's patient advisor, the friend whose opinion is sought sparingly but trusted absolutely. The reach of seven is not noisy, but it is unusually firm.

The shadow of seven is detachment. The wisdom that becomes withdrawal, the privacy that becomes isolation. The discipline of a Driver seven is to honour the inwardness without losing the cord to ordinary life — to keep depth in dialogue with company.

Focus III · the life path

Conductor Number 3

引導數 — the Life Number, the path the life is conducted along

The full date sums and reduces to the Conductor Number — the path the life is being guided along, the larger arc the years bend toward.

The digits of 16 May 1953 sum to thirty, which reduces to three. Three in the Lo Shu reading is expansion, communication, and the unfolding of vision. It is the number of those who articulate, who teach, who give visible form to inner understanding.

The Driver–Conductor pair of seven and three describes a private depth that comes out as articulate teaching. What looks from outside as advisory work is, on the inside, the natural overflow of long inward study. The chart is shaped to translate, not to merely accumulate.

The risk of a Conductor three is breadth without depth — talk that becomes its own end. The discipline is to keep the speaking grounded in the patient seven beneath, so that what is said carries weight rather than only volume.

Focus IV · the Feng Shui directional number

Kua Number 2 · Kun

West Group, Earth element — the receptive, the patient, the supportive

The Kua is the Feng Shui directional number — the trigram of the chart, calculated separately for males and females from the year of birth.

For a male born in 1953, the year-digit-sum (5+3=8) subtracted from ten gives two. Kua two belongs to the West Group of the eight directions, governed by the trigram Kun (☷) — three broken lines, the most receptive of the eight gua. Kun is the Earth itself: nurturing, patient, supportive, grounded in human relationship.

Favourable directions for a Kua two (male, West Group) are north-east for prosperity (Sheng Chi), west for health (Tien Yi), north-west for love and family (Yan Nian), and south-west for personal growth (Fu Wei). Less favourable are north, south, east, and south-east.

These directions traditionally guide the orientation of one's desk, bed, and front door. Read here as indications, not prescriptions — they describe the directions the chart is most at ease with, not commandments about how a house must be arranged.

Focus V · the year of birth

癸巳 · Yin Water Snake

The most contemplative of the Snake variants

1953 is the year of the Yin Water Snake — Gui Si — the most philosophical and inwardly powerful of the six Snake variants in the sixty-year cycle.

Water Snakes are read as quietly wise, intuitive, and inwardly powerful. They are drawn to study, to meaning, to the long observation of human nature. Their will is hidden under a calm exterior; their decisions, when finally made, are unusually firm.

Compatibility within the Chinese zodiac places the Snake's strongest natural alliances with the Ox and the Rooster, forming the San He triangle of the three harmonies of the Snake. Most testing dynamics are with the Tiger and the Pig — opposite or perpendicular signs in the cycle.

The lucky elements for a Water Snake are Metal and Water; the lucky colours are black, deep blue, gold, and white. The sign reinforces the Driver-seven and Conductor-three pattern of the chart — Snake's quietness and Water's depth amplify the same inwardness the Lo Shu grid already shows.

Focus VI · the arrow present

The Arrow of Determination

1 · 5 · 9 — the centre column fully present, doubled at the source

When all three numbers of a row, column, or diagonal of the Lo Shu grid appear in the chart, an arrow forms — a fully present line of strength, traditionally read as a gift the chart carries from birth.

The vertical centre column of the grid — North 1, Centre 5, South 9 — is fully present in your chart, and both 1 and 5 appear twice. This is the Arrow of Determination, sometimes called the Plane of Will. It is one of the most powerful arrows the Lo Shu can show.

It signals extraordinary perseverance, the ability to keep going long after others stop, and the rare gift of seeing a project through to completion. Doubled at the source (the centre 5) and doubled at the foundation (the north 1), the arrow here is unusually strong — the will is reinforced both at the seat and at its grounding.

No row, column, or diagonal in your chart is entirely empty, which means there is no full arrow of weakness. The grid has gaps — the absent palaces are 2, 4, 7, and 8 — but no plane of complete absence. The reading is taken as a balanced constitution with cultivable, rather than fundamental, growth areas.

Synthesis · the Wu Xing Portrait

Unforced authority — five elements, no silence

The Lo Shu reading describes a person whose grid is centred on a doubled five — the Emperor's seat — and a doubled one — clear self-expression — held together by the full Arrow of Determination running North-Centre-South.

All five Wu Xing elements are represented in the chart. Water is strong (1, doubled). Earth is strong (5, doubled, reinforced by Kua 2). Wood is present (3). Fire is present (9). Metal is present (6). The chart is therefore elementally complete — none of the five is silent, even though some are quieter than others.

The Driver is the contemplative seven; the Conductor is the expansive three; the Kua is the receptive Kun-Earth two of the West Group; the Year Pillar is Yin Water Snake — quiet, philosophical, deeply observant, and unusually firm once decided. The lessons of the chart — partnerships, structure, empathy, formal organisation — sit in the absent palaces 2, 4, 7, and 8, and are areas to cultivate rather than fundamental gaps.

It is the chart of unforced authority. A centred individual with strong will and a complete elemental field, whose preferred mode is depth rather than display, and whose presence is the kind that other people quietly orient around.
