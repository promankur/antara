Introduction

What this report is about

The same person, read through five different traditions of number

This report is not a sixth numerology system. It is a combined reading that takes the same name and date of birth, runs them through the five lineage traditions covered in the rest of this set — Pythagorean, Chaldean, Kabbalah, Tamil and Lo Shu — and brings the results into a single comparative view.

Each tradition computes a different set of numbers from the same data. The numbers do not always agree. Where they do, the convergence is unusually telling. Where they differ, each tradition is adding something the others did not see — and the differences are mostly differences of vocabulary rather than disagreements about the person.

The defining and somewhat surprising finding for this chart is that three numbers converge cleanly across three or more independent traditions — the path number three, the day number seven, and the name number two. Where five readings made independently still arrive at the same trinity, the trinity is the part of the chart to listen to most closely.

How to read this report

Structure & method

Convergence first; each tradition's distinctive contribution after

This report is structured so that each focus aspect lives on its own page. The first focus shows the convergence mandala — the centrepiece of the report. The second names the convergent core. The next five pages take each tradition in turn and describe what it adds beyond that core. The final page is a unified portrait.

The contents page that follows lists every focus with a hyperlink. In a browser or PDF you may tap any title to jump directly to the relevant page. Each individual lineage report — Pythagorean, Chaldean, Kabbalah, Tamil, Lo Shu — is also available separately for deeper reading.

A standard note applies to the whole. Numerology, in all five traditions, is interpretive rather than scientifically validated. This profile is offered as character reflection, not as predictive, medical, financial or legal counsel. The reader is the final authority on the reading.

Reading time
Around twenty-five minutes at an unhurried pace.

The Convergent Core · 3 · 7 · 2

The three numbers reached by three or more independent lineages

Where five readings made independently still arrive at the same trinity, the trinity is the part of the chart to listen to most closely.

The seven of the day. Pythagoras (Birthday 7), Chaldean (Psychic 7), Tamil (Mulank 7), and Lo Shu (Driver 7) all read the sixteenth as a contemplative seven — the inner scholar, the seeker. Kabbalah does not compute a day-number, but places the moment of birth in the post-Shavuot window of received study, which is the same note in another key.

The three of the path. Pythagoras (Life Path 3), Chaldean (Destiny 3), Tamil (Bhagyank 3), and Lo Shu (Conductor 3) all reduce the full date to the same Jupiter / expressive / teaching number. The path is to communicate, to expand, to guide.

The two of the name. Chaldean (Name 38 → 2 / Moon), Tamil (Namank 2 / Chandra), Kabbalah's reduced inner letter (Beth, 2), and Lo Shu's Kua 2 (Kun, Earth) all land on two. The way other people experience this name and direction is gentle, perceptive, soft of presence.

Pythagorean Lens

What the Greek school adds beyond the convergent core

Pythagoras adds the architecture — Expression, Soul Urge, Personality, and Maturity — none of which the other four traditions compute in the same form.

The Greek lens contributes Expression 4 (the Builder), Soul Urge 8 (the inner ambition), Personality 5 (the mobile public face), and Maturity 7 (the doubled inwardness emerging late in life). Together these explain the productive seriousness that the day-born seven alone would not predict — the chart is not only contemplative; it is also constructive, ambitious in private, sociable in public, and sage by evening.

The doubled seven — once at the Birthday, once at the Maturity — is the Greek lens's most striking observation. The contemplative undercurrent that the four other traditions also note is, in the Pythagorean reading, the explicit destination of the later years.

Chaldean Lens

What the Babylonian school adds beyond the convergent core

Chaldean adds the karmic compounds — numbers like 16, 38 and 30 read in their own right before reduction — and names the planet behind every number.

The compound 16 beneath the Psychic seven (sometimes called the Shattered Citadel) is read as karmic awakening through reversal — a warning against pride balanced by an unusual capacity for spiritual depth. It is preserved as a compound and not visible in Pythagorean or Lo Shu reductions.

The compound 38 beneath the Name two reduces through eleven, itself a master vibration. The Chaldean lens reads this as a gentle outer presence carrying unusually strong inner perception — a name that listens before it answers, and sees more than it lets on.

And Chaldean names the planet behind each number — Ketu within, Jupiter ahead, Moon at the surface, Saturn beneath as the Saturday day-lord. The reading becomes locatable in the planetary scheme rather than only in character.

Focus V · the Hebrew addition

Kabbalistic Lens

What the Tree of Life adds beyond the convergent core

Kabbalah lifts the same arithmetic onto the Tree of Life and turns the answer from a label into a path with neighbours.

What Pythagoras calls a Builder Four, Kabbalah translates onto the Tree as the soul-letter Resh — twenty in gematria — corresponding to The Sun in the Tarot path system. The path runs from Hod (intellect, language) through Yesod (foundation, the unconscious). The native does not just have a destiny number; they walk a specific path on a specific diagram.

The reduced inner letter is Beth — two, The Magician — the focused will that turns idea into form. Beth's path runs from Kether (pure intent) to Binah (structured understanding). The chart becomes architectural rather than only descriptive: ideas held in pure form, methodically built down into reality.

And the Hebrew calendar moment of birth is the second of Sivan, 5713 — the window immediately after Shavuot, the festival of the giving of the Torah. Births in this window are read as carrying a particular relationship to study and transmission. None of the other four traditions compute this.

Focus VI · the Vedic addition

Tamil Lens

What the Tamil & Vedic tradition adds beyond the convergent core

Tamil grounds the chart in planetary lords and in sacred geography. Where the other traditions describe character, Tamil specifies day-lord, ruling planet, and the colour the place of birth gives.

The Tamil lens shares the Mulank-7, Bhagyank-3, Namank-2 trinity with the convergent core, but ties each number to its planetary lord — Ketu (the inward seeker), Guru (the teacher), Chandra (the Moon, sensitive presence). The day of birth, Saturday, brings Shani the patient planet beneath everything. The chart is therefore a four-planet portrait, not a three-number one.

And Tamil reads place. Chakrata sits in Uttarakhand, the Himalayan state Indian tradition calls Devbhoomi — the land of the gods. Birth in such a region is read as aligning the early energy field with stillness, height, and shastric depth. None of the other four traditions count place at all. For a Mulank seven, this is unusually well-aligned: the place reinforces the inwardness of the chart rather than fighting it.

Focus VII · the Chinese addition

Lo Shu Lens

What the Chinese magic-square tradition adds beyond the convergent core

Lo Shu draws the same data into a 3×3 magic square and reads what is present, what is absent, and what is doubled.

The chart's nine palaces show 1 and 5 doubled, with 3, 6, and 9 each singly present, while 2, 4, 7, and 8 are absent. The full vertical column 1·5·9 is present, forming the Arrow of Determination — a powerful line of perseverance. No row, column, or diagonal is entirely empty, meaning there is no full arrow of weakness; the chart is read as a balanced constitution with cultivable rather than fundamental gaps.

Lo Shu also reads through Wu Xing — the five-element balance. All five elements (Water, Wood, Fire, Earth, Metal) are represented, with Water and Earth dominating. The chart is therefore elementally complete — no element is silent.

And Lo Shu adds the Kua direction (2, Kun-Earth, West Group) and the Year Pillar (Yin Water Snake), placing the chart on the Chinese geomantic and zodiacal maps. The directions favourable for orientation of one's daily life are NE, W, NW, SW.

Focus VIII · the portrait

The Unified Portrait

Five lineages, five vocabularies, one chord — earned wisdom that listens before it speaks

Across all five lineages, the same person is described — and the differences are mostly in vocabulary, not in subject.

A contemplative inner Seven, walking a Jupiter / Three path of teaching and expansion, wearing a Moon / Two name that other people find gentle and perceptive. Saturn beneath, giving the dignity of long time. Pythagorean adds that this person is also a Builder Four with a private ambition for mastery. Chaldean names the planets behind the numbers — Ketu within, Jupiter ahead, Moon at the surface, Saturn as day-lord beneath. Kabbalah lifts the same numbers onto the Tree as Resh and Beth — the Sun and the Magician. Tamil grounds them in planetary lords and in Devbhoomi. LoShu draws them into the magic square and finds the full Arrow of Determination running through the centre column.

The defining quality, common to all five readings, is earned wisdom that listens before it speaks. Productive on the surface; contemplative underneath; slow to peak and long to remain. The Personal Year 2026, by both Pythagorean and Tamil reckoning a Four / Rahu year, asks for consolidation and the careful shaping of what has already been built.

Five traditions, five vocabularies, one chord — Three of expression, Seven of contemplation, Two of presence, held together by Saturn's long time and lit by the Sun of Resh.

Tree of Life
